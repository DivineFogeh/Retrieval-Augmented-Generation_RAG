Title: Transformer (deep learning architecture)
URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformer_(deep_learning_architecture)

The transformer is a deep learning architecture introduced in the 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need" by Vaswani et al. at Google Brain. It has become the dominant architecture for natural language processing and has been applied successfully to computer vision, audio processing, and multi-modal tasks.

Before transformers, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and long short-term memory networks (LSTMs) were the standard for sequence modeling. These models process sequences token by token, making them slow to train on long sequences and prone to forgetting information from early in the sequence. Transformers address these limitations by processing all tokens in parallel and using attention mechanisms to directly model relationships between any two positions in the sequence, regardless of distance.

The core innovation of the transformer is the self-attention mechanism. Self-attention allows each token in a sequence to attend to all other tokens and compute a weighted representation based on their relevance. For each token, three vectors are computed: a Query (Q), a Key (K), and a Value (V). Attention scores are calculated as the scaled dot product of Q and K, normalized with a softmax. These scores weight the Value vectors to produce the output. Multi-head attention runs several attention operations in parallel with different learned projections, allowing the model to attend to information from different representation subspaces simultaneously.

Positional encoding is added to the input embeddings to give the model information about the order of tokens, since self-attention is permutation-invariant. The original transformer used fixed sinusoidal positional encodings. Modern models often use learned positional embeddings or relative positional encodings like Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE), which is used in LLaMA and other modern LLMs.

The transformer encoder processes an input sequence and produces a contextual representation of each token. BERT-style models use an encoder-only architecture and are pre-trained with masked language modeling. The transformer decoder generates an output sequence autoregressively, one token at a time, attending to previously generated tokens (via masked self-attention) and to the encoder output (via cross-attention). GPT-style models use a decoder-only architecture and are pre-trained with causal (left-to-right) language modeling. Encoder-decoder models like T5 and BART use both components and are well-suited for sequence-to-sequence tasks like translation and summarization.

The scaling of transformer models has proven extremely effective. Research has shown that model performance improves predictably as a power-law function of model size, training data, and compute (these are known as scaling laws). This insight has driven the development of increasingly large models, from GPT-2 (1.5B parameters) to GPT-3 (175B parameters) and beyond, leading to the era of large language models.
